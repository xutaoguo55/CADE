## Abstract

In rare-disease bulk transcriptomics, matched single-cell references are usually unavailable while cell-composition shifts can dominate apparent differential expression (DE). CADE (Composition-Aware Differential Expression) addresses this gap by converting the paired unadjusted and composition-adjusted limma coefficients of every gene into a per-gene **Composition Confounding Index (CCI)** that ranks coefficients from adjustment-stable to adjustment-sensitive, alongside signed attenuation/amplification/reversal labels. The ranking is audited with marker-dropout bootstrap, label permutation, threshold scans, stabilised-denominator diagnostics and an isometric log-ratio (ILR) coordinate-sensitivity layer. We evaluated CADE on six benchmarks: a synthetic composition-bias gradient (AUROC 0.996 at 100% bias versus 0.902 for unadjusted limma), a PBMC ground-truth simulation (mean r = 0.993), a sparse-marker scRNA-seq stress test, two independent sepsis cohorts (GSE28750, GSE66099) and a MAS pseudobulk validation against scRNA-seq ground truth (mean r = 0.779, 7/8 cell types p < 0.001). Applied to familial hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis peripheral blood mononuclear cells (GSE26050; n = 11 FHL versus 30 controls), CADE ranked a 20-gene ferroptosis/iron/immune panel and identified *SLC7A11* as the most stable candidate (rank-stability probability 0.835) and *STAT1*, *GCLM*, *HMOX1* and *GPX4* as composition-sensitive. **Take-home:** users with small cohorts and no matched single-cell reference can run CADE to obtain an auditable per-gene composition-sensitivity ranking and a reproducible code/data package under the MIT license. All FHL findings remain hypothesis-generating because of small sample size.

**Keywords:** CADE; Composition Confounding Index; coefficient-sensitivity ranking; differential expression; bulk transcriptomics; rare disease; small sample size; familial hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis; ferroptosis



## Introduction

Bulk tissue transcriptomes are weighted averages of expression across constituent cell types. When disease alters cell-type abundance, a standard differential-expression (DE) coefficient can combine at least two components: regulation within a cell type and shifts in the mixture of cell types that already express the gene. In inflammatory, neoplastic, and degenerative conditions, such composition shifts can reverse apparent effect sizes, generate false positives, and obscure composition-stable disease-associated signals (1–4).

The challenge is particularly acute in rare-disease transcriptomics. Sample sizes are often too small for high-dimensional adjustment, matched single-cell references are frequently unavailable, and disease-associated changes in cellularity may themselves be biologically meaningful. Under these constraints, the practical downstream question is not simply whether an adjusted DE table can be produced; it is whether each reported disease coefficient is stable enough to justify follow-up. A reference-free approach that ranks coefficient sensitivity can fill a practical interpretability gap for small-*N* bulk studies.

Established deconvolution methods—CIBERSORTx (1), EPIC (2), Bisque (3) and MuSiC (4)—are powerful when appropriate references exist, but rare-disease studies, including familial hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (FHL), often require an additional strategy because disease-, tissue- and platform-matched reference profiles are not always available. Marker-gene scoring offers a reference-free alternative. The mean expression of curated marker genes serves as an internally calibrated proxy for relative lineage abundance, and marker-score or surrogate-variable covariates have been used in regression models for many years (5,6). What these approaches typically provide is a *single* composition-adjusted DE table. They do not provide a per-gene answer to the question most relevant for follow-up: which disease coefficients are stable, attenuated, amplified, direction-sensitive, or representation-sensitive after adjustment?

CADE targets this interpretability layer. It defines a per-gene coefficient-sensitivity estimand (CCI), reports signed coefficient-change categories, and calibrates rankings via marker-dropout bootstrap, label permutation, threshold scans, stabilised-denominator diagnostics and ILR coordinate-sensitivity analysis for closed weights. The novelty of CADE is not the use of marker covariates per se, but the standardised sensitivity-analysis layer that turns a single adjusted DE table into an auditable per-gene ranking with rank-stability diagnostics. Cell-type-specific DE methods such as TOAST (7) and related approaches (8) estimate whether a gene is DE *within* a specific cell type, which is a complementary, not competing, question to CADE's bulk-level sensitivity ranking.

FHL is a life-threatening autosomal recessive hyperinflammatory syndrome caused by defects in lymphocyte cytotoxicity genes, most commonly *PRF1* (9,10). It is a demanding worked example for CADE because the available public transcriptomic dataset is small (n = 11 FHL versus 30 controls), matched single-cell reference data for the same tissue are unavailable, and the disease is expected to involve substantial shifts in peripheral blood immune lineages. The original GSE26050 study, which profiled PBMCs from untreated children with active FHL, reported dysregulation of cytotoxicity, inflammasome and interferon-pathway genes (9); CADE re-analyses this dataset to ask which of those findings are composition-robust. FHL is also relevant to iron metabolism: extreme hyperferritinemia is a diagnostic clue (11), and ferroptosis is an iron-dependent regulated cell-death process linked to cystine transport (*SLC7A11*) (14), *GPX4* function (16), and mitochondrial iron handling (17,28). The ferroptosis core (12), defence (13) and NRF2-associated (15) pathways are key regulatory nodes. The JAK-STAT pathway, particularly *STAT3*, is a central signalling hub in HLH downstream of IFNγ, IL-6 and IL-10 (10,18,19); standard first-line HLH therapy with etoposide and dexamethasone was established by the HLH-2004 trial (27). Ruxolitinib (18,20) and emapalumab (10) have shown clinical activity in HLH, providing translational motivation for characterising JAK-STAT gene expression alongside ferroptosis pathways.

This study has one primary methodological aim and one illustrative application. The methodological aim is to develop and benchmark CADE/CCI as a single-cell-reference-free framework for ranking the sensitivity of bulk DE coefficients to marker-derived composition adjustment. The illustrative application applies CADE to FHL PBMC (GSE26050; n = 11 FHL vs 30 controls) to rank ferroptosis-, iron- and immune-related genes by sensitivity to marker-derived composition adjustment, while explicitly flagging hypothesis-generating limitations including small sample size and heterogeneous genotypes.



## Methods

### Study design and data overview

This study was designed for method development and validation. CADE was first evaluated in benchmarks where the degree of composition bias was known or partly known, and then applied to a rare-disease bulk transcriptomic dataset with biologically plausible composition shifts. The overall workflow is shown in Figure 1.

For the application tier, we re-analysed GSE26050, a publicly available peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) transcriptomic dataset profiled on the Affymetrix Human Genome U133 Plus 2.0 Array (GPL570). The dataset comprises PBMCs from 11 untreated children with active FHL and 30 healthy paediatric controls, processed by RMA normalisation and DWD batch correction as deposited in GEO (9). Sample characteristics: n = 11 FHL vs 30 healthy controls, all PBMCs. According to GEO metadata, the FHL samples were collected before treatment initiation (untreated); however, individual-level treatment-status documentation is not available in the public repository, and the original study relied on self-reported treatment-naïve status. Individual-level genotype data are also unavailable, precluding subgroup analysis by genetic subtype. Using the observed median residual standard deviation from GSE26050 (σ̂ = 0.42 log2 units), the minimum detectable |logFC| at 80% power (two-sided α = 0.05) for n = 11 vs 30 is approximately 0.76 log2 units; this power bound contextualises non-significant panel genes such as *FTL*, *GPX4*, *HMOX1* and *NCOA4*.

### Data preprocessing

The GSE26050 series matrix was downloaded using GEOquery (v2.74.1) (21). According to GEO metadata, the deposited data had been processed with Robust Multi-array Average normalisation in GeneSpring 7.3, median-centred and corrected for batch structure using Distance Weighted Discrimination. Probe sets (n = 54 675) were mapped to gene symbols using the `hgu133plus2.db` annotation package (v3.13.0). For genes represented by multiple probe sets, the probe set with the highest interquartile range was retained, yielding 20 812 unique genes. DE analysis used the `limma` R package (v3.62.1) with empirical Bayes moderation; Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) FDR correction was applied across all 20 812 genes (FDR threshold 0.05). A file-level reproducibility check against an independently generated table from the same series matrix yielded median logFC deviation 0.000 and mean absolute deviation 0.186 across 74 common genes (64/74 within 0.5 log2 units; Supplementary Table S1).

### CADE: Composition-Aware Differential Expression

**Working model.** Let *Y*_{gi} be the bulk expression of gene *g* in sample *i*, and let *w*_{ki} denote the marker-derived relative composition weight for cell type or lineage *k*. CADE uses the additive working model

Y_{gi} = α_g + x_i β_g + Σ_{k=1..K} w_{ki} γ_{gk} + ε_{gi}, ε_{gi} ~ N(0, σ_g²)

where *x*_i encodes disease status. The coefficient from a model without the *w* covariates estimates the unadjusted bulk group effect, and the coefficient from the model including selected *w* covariates estimates the group effect conditional on the marker-derived composition weights. This model supports a coefficient-change analysis, not a formal causal decomposition. In particular, disease may alter marker expression within cell types, relevant cell types may be unmeasured, and treatment or batch effects may affect both marker weights and gene expression.

**Stage 1: marker-derived composition weights.** Initial weights are estimated from mean marker expression per sample and normalised by softmax. We distinguish two execution modes: **CADE-lite** uses a single softmax normalisation of mean marker expression and no iterative refinement; **CADE-full** adds an optional refinement that alternates between (a) estimating marker-gene cell-type profiles by regression against the current weights and (b) updating per-sample weights using constrained quadratic programming (QP) with non-negativity and sum-to-one constraints. The CADE-full QP step is a standard quadratic program with linear inequality constraints, which is guaranteed to converge to the global optimum of each sub-problem (the cell-type profile regression is also a global optimum), but the overall alternation is a block-coordinate descent with no global convergence guarantee. **Empirically**, the algorithm converges in 32 iterations for the FHL application (final δ=8.22×10⁻⁷); across 5 PBMC benchmark replicates, the final δ ranged from 10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁵ with no observed oscillations. Iteration terminates at max |Δweight| < 10⁻⁶ or 50 iterations. Because the input is log-transformed microarray expression and marker baselines differ across lineages, the resulting weights are relative within-study quantities rather than absolute biological proportions.

**Stage 2: composition-adjusted DE.** A limma linear model is fit with the top-*k* most variable marker-derived weights as covariates: `expr ~ group + weight_1 + ... + weight_k`. An unadjusted model (`expr ~ group`) is fit in parallel. Both models use empirical Bayes moderation.

**Stage 3: CCI coefficient-sensitivity spectrum.** For each gene with |logFC_unadj| > 0.1,

CCI = clip([0, 1], |logFC_unadj − logFC_adj| / |logFC_unadj|)

The 0.1 denominator threshold was chosen because, at n = 11 with typical limma standard errors of 0.3–0.5 log2 units, |logFC_unadj| < 0.1 corresponds to coefficients within ~0.2–0.3 standard errors of zero, a region where the ratio estimator becomes dominated by denominator uncertainty (delta-method variance diverges as |μ_Y| → 0). **Empirical threshold-sensitivity analysis** (Supplementary Figure S4A): across τ ∈ {0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50} the Spearman rank correlation of the 19-gene FHL panel CCI ordering remained ρ = 1.0, indicating that the qualitative ranking is robust to the exact threshold choice. A stabilised CCI variant (denominator regularised by `max(|logFC_unadj|, 0.1)`) is provided in code for low-baseline cases, but the primary reported CCI is the legacy formula. The clipped range groups attenuation and amplification together, so CADE always reports the signed coefficient change (attenuation, amplification, reversal, stable) alongside CCI.

**Compositional-geometry sensitivity layer.** Marker-derived weights are closed relative quantities: increasing one weight necessarily decreases at least one other. CADE therefore implements an optional ILR mode (22,23) that transforms the closed weight matrix into a Helmert-subspace basis of orthonormal balances and refits the adjusted model with the top-variance ILR coordinates rather than the raw weights. ILR coordinates are orthonormal balances, so CADE-ILR complements lineage-level interpretation with a coordinate-system sensitivity analysis.

**Uncertainty quantification.** Marker sensitivity was assessed with 200 bootstrap iterations, each randomly dropping 20% of markers per cell type and re-estimating weights, adjusted logFC, CCI and CCI rank. Bootstrap summaries report CCI medians, percentile intervals, rank intervals, and the probability that a gene remains among the five lowest-CCI panel genes. The 200-iteration count and 20% dropout rate were chosen based on a stability scan (Supplementary Figure S6): CCI mean changed by < 0.01 between n=200 and n=400, and the rank-stability probability changed by < 0.02 between 10% and 30% dropout. A convergence test showed that mean CCI and rank-stability probability for the ferroptosis-panel genes changed by less than 0.02 when increasing from n=100 to n=200, and less than 0.01 when further increasing to n=400, indicating that 200 iterations are sufficient for stable point estimates; doubling to 400 does not qualitatively alter the conclusions. The 20% dropout rate was chosen to balance signal retention (80% of markers retained) with perturbation variability (each bootstrap uses a different marker subset), and the rank-stability probability changed by < 0.02 between 10% and 30% dropout. To check stochastic reproducibility, we repeated the CADE-ILR bootstrap with three independent random seeds (42, 2026, 31415; 200 iterations per seed) and summarised across-seed stability of priority-gene ranks. We also computed Wilson 95% confidence intervals for the rank-stability probabilities; the CI for *SLC7A11*'s top-5 probability (0.835) was [0.787, 0.873]. Wilson 95% CIs were computed for all 20 panel genes (Supplementary Table S8) and confirm the ranking conclusions: *SLC7A11* [0.777, 0.880], *TNF* [0.675, 0.796] and *IL1B* [0.628, 0.755] are robustly low-CCI candidates, while *SLC25A37* [0.001, 0.028] and *STAT1* [0.005, 0.043] are robustly high-CCI; the remaining 15 genes have 95% CIs that include intermediate values, justifying the rank-stability probability as the more reliable single-number summary than the point estimate alone. For stabilised CCI, we report approximate delta-method intervals using limma-derived standard errors and a conservative zero-covariance approximation between unadjusted and adjusted group coefficients. Label permutation (1000 shuffles) was used to estimate empirical null distributions for the FHL panel.

**Statistical properties of CCI as a ratio estimator.** CCI is a ratio of correlated estimated coefficients and is most informative when the unadjusted coefficient is sufficiently separated from zero. A delta-method approximation at n = 11 shows that genes with |logFC_unadj| < 0.3 can have high relative uncertainty in CCI even when the absolute coefficient shift is stable. CCI is therefore interpreted as a continuous sensitivity ranking; classification ranges (<0.2, 0.2–0.5, >0.5) are descriptive guides rather than statistical decision boundaries. In the PBMC synthetic benchmark, simple percentile bootstrap intervals achieved empirical coverage of 89.3% (low-CCI), 91.7% (intermediate-CCI) and 93.1% (high-CCI) across 50 replicate simulations, all below nominal 95% but informative for ranking stability. **Bias-correction note:** the archived bootstrap CIs are percentile intervals without bias correction; bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) bootstrap was not implemented in the archived code because CCI's ratio structure makes BCa non-trivial. The under-coverage (89–93% vs 95%) is consistent with the ratio-estimator's known bias toward larger values when the denominator is small, but for the purpose of gene ranking rather than precise CI coverage, the simple percentile intervals are adequate. The rank-stability probabilities are robust to bias because they are based on relative rank positions rather than point estimates.

**FHL application parameters.** CADE was applied to GSE26050 (PBMCs) using eight curated immune lineage marker sets (CD8 T cells, CD4 T cells, NK cells, B cells, monocytes, macrophages, neutrophils, erythrocytes; 7–9 markers each; full gene lists in Supplementary Table S2). Markers were curated from three sources: (i) the LM22 signature matrix used by CIBERSORT (1) for immune lineages, (ii) lineage-specific canonical markers from the Immunological Genome Project (ImmGen) for neutrophil and monocyte populations, and (iii) additional granulocyte and erythroid-lineage markers based on published PBMC transcriptomic atlases. Because GSE26050 samples are PBMCs, the LM22 adult peripheral blood markers are tissue-appropriate for the main immune lineages, while erythrocyte markers reflect erythrocyte transcripts present in PBMC preparations rather than bone-marrow erythropoiesis. The top four most-variable marker-derived weights were included as covariates. The choice of k = 4 was guided by the variance structure of the estimated weights: the top four weights captured 78% of total weight variance, and k ∈ {3, 4, 5} produced highly concordant CCI rankings (mean pairwise Spearman ρ > 0.94). We caution that k should remain small relative to sample size (k < n/10) to avoid overfitting the adjusted model. The eight marker sets contain 66 entries but only 58 unique genes; eight markers are shared across related lineages. A deduplicated variant in which each shared marker was assigned to only one set produced modestly lower AUROC (0.779 vs 0.837) and only moderate panel CCI concordance (Spearman ρ = 0.55), supporting retention of the original overlapping configuration.

**Implementation.** CADE is implemented in R with dependencies on `limma`, `quadprog`, and base R. The implementation provides `estimate_proportions_cade()` for marker-derived weight estimation, `cade_de_analysis()` for paired DE and CCI computation (with `composition_transform = "ilr"` for ILR covariates), and wrappers for marker sensitivity, rank-stability analysis and permutation calibration. The software outputs standardised tables containing unadjusted and adjusted coefficients, legacy and stabilised CCI, signed coefficient-change category, selected covariates, bootstrap intervals, rank-stability probabilities and permutation null summaries. The standalone `cade_ilr_uncertainty.R` script generates the CADE-ILR tables and figure. The submission package includes project-relative scripts, dependency notes, `renv.lock` and a documented end-to-end run order to support reproducibility.

### CADE benchmark framework

**Dose-response synthetic benchmark.** Synthetic bulk transcriptomic data (3000 genes × 44 samples × 5 cell types) were generated. Cell-type-specific profiles were drawn from normal distributions, with marker genes (20 across 5 cell types) assigned elevated cell-type specificity (+3.0 log2 units). True DE genes (n = 50, non-overlapping with markers) received composition-independent group-level regulation (logFC ≈ 1.5). Composition bias was varied across 5 levels (α = 0%, 10%, 25%, 50%, 100%) by interpolating between balanced and FHL-like skewed proportions. Three independent replicates per bias level.

**PBMC ground-truth benchmark.** CADE was evaluated on a PBMC-inspired synthetic dataset with realistic immune-cell marker configurations (8 cell types, 96 unique literature-curated markers, 5000 genes, 90 samples). Marker-derived weights showed strong per-cell-type rank agreement with simulated ground-truth proportions (mean r = 0.993, range 0.981–0.998, RMSE = 0.077, MAE = 0.065). Linear marker-mean initialization alone achieved comparable per-cell-type correlation (mean r = 0.993), confirming that marker quality dominates over QP refinement.

**Real scRNA-seq pseudobulk benchmark.** The Monaco Immune Reference benchmark tested CADE in a sparse-marker, modest-effect setting (18 markers, 5 cell types). Under balanced composition, unadjusted limma achieved the highest AUROC, as expected when no composition bias is present. Once composition bias reached ≥50%, CADE outperformed standard limma. The benchmark also assessed CCI's discriminatory ability (separation of composition-driven from cell-intrinsic DE) and weight-proportion correlation (limited marker availability in this sparse benchmark reduces correlation but does not impair CCI separation). Matched-nnls signature-matrix deconvolution (using the same cell-type profiles to construct the reference) served as the ideal matched-reference comparator, distinct from CIBERSORTx which uses nu-SVR with an independently derived signature matrix.

**External validation in sepsis whole blood (GSE28750, GSE66099).** CADE was applied to two independent sepsis cohorts profiled on the same GPL570 platform as the FHL data. GSE28750 (whole blood, n = 10 patients vs 20 healthy controls) is a dataset with documented cell-composition shifts (neutrophil expansion, lymphoid depletion (24)); cohort-level CCI distribution, adjusted vs raw logFC, and the 20-gene sepsis-signalling CCI waterfall are in Supplementary Figure S2. GSE66099 (paediatric SIRS/sepsis/septic shock whole blood, n = 229 vs 47 controls, ICU Day 1) provides a larger-sample replication (25); per-gene outputs and cross-context CCI are in Supplementary Table S6.

**MAS pseudobulk validation with scRNA-seq ground truth.** Synthetic bulk RNA-seq data were constructed from 14 cell-type-specific pseudobulk count files derived from a Macrophage Activation Syndrome scRNA-seq dataset (GSE207633, 10x Genomics). Pseudobulk counts were aggregated by cell type for each of 25 samples spanning 5 conditions (Active, LungDisease, MAS, Healthy, Inactive), then summed to produce a synthetic bulk matrix (13 145 genes × 25 samples). Ground truth cell-type composition was computed from per-cell-type pseudobulk library sizes, normalised to proportions per sample. CADE-lite was applied using 8 LM22-derived marker gene sets (7–9 markers each; benchmark outputs in Supplementary Tables S5, MAS validation outputs in Supplementary Table S6). CADE's 8 broad immune lineages were mapped to the 14 finer-grained scRNA-seq cell types by summing the latter (e.g., CD4_T_Cells + CD4_Naive + CD4_TCM → CD4 T cells). All seven evaluable cell types achieved significant rank agreement with ground truth (Pearson r range 0.675–0.915; mean 0.779, median 0.775; all p < 0.001; per-cell-type scatter plots in Supplementary Figure S3).

### Permutation testing for CCI calibration

To assess whether observed CCI values differ from null expectations, group labels were permuted 1000 times (maintaining the 11:30 FHL:HC ratio), and the CADE DE step was re-run on each permuted dataset. Marker-derived weight estimation is group-independent and was performed once on the original data, shared across all permutations. Two-sided empirical P-values were computed and BH correction was applied across testable genes. The permutation design shares weights across shuffles, so the empirical null reflects DE-step uncertainty conditional on fixed weights. The full permutation distribution (Supplementary Table S4) is reported as a calibration diagnostic for the panel-level CCI distribution; it is not a formal hypothesis test (full results in Supplementary Table S4).

### Immune cell-type scoring, gene-set and supporting analyses

Immune cell-type signatures were scored using curated marker gene sets derived from the LM22 (CIBERSORT) signature matrix and published immune cell atlases. For each of eight lineages (CD8 T cells, CD4 T cells, NK cells, B cells, monocytes, macrophages, neutrophils, erythrocytes), 7–9 canonical marker genes were selected. The mean log2 expression of marker genes per sample was used as a relative lineage score.

Gene-set enrichment was tested with CAMERA (limma::cameraPR) on the ranked limma t-statistics. GSVA (v2.4.9) was used to summarise per-sample pathway activity. WGCNA (v1.72-5) used the top 5000 most variable genes with signed network, soft-thresholding power β = 9 (scale-free R² = 0.856), minimum module size 30, merge cut height 0.25. Target-gene signature scoring used curated 10–14 target sets for seven TFs (STAT3, NFKB1, NFE2L2, STAT1, HIF1A, IRF1, TP53). All R packages and versions are listed in `code/DEPENDENCIES.md` and locked in `renv.lock`.

## Results

### CADE benchmark validation

Benchmark results are summarised in Figure 2 and Tables 1A–1D. On the dose-response synthetic benchmark, CADE maintained AUROC ≥ 0.996 at all bias levels, while standard limma declined from 1.000 at 0% bias to 0.902 at 100% bias (Table 1A). True DE genes showed consistently low CCI (mean 0.038–0.334), whereas background genes showed higher CCI (mean 0.354–0.696); the 0%-bias background mean of 0.354 establishes the noise floor arising from random covariate–expression correlations and degrees-of-freedom loss.

On the PBMC-inspired benchmark (96 unique markers, 8 cell types), CADE weights agreed strongly with simulated ground-truth proportions (mean r = 0.993, RMSE = 0.077; Table 1B / scale shrinkage mild). On the Monaco Immune Reference benchmark (Table 1B), CADE outperformed standard limma at bias ≥50% (AUROC 0.967–0.734 vs 0.867–0.572), at the cost of reduced power at 0% bias (0.938 vs 1.000). CCI correctly separated composition-driven from cell-intrinsic DE (mean CCI 0.54–0.82 vs 0.24–0.53). CIBERSORTx-style matched-nnls with a perfectly matched reference achieved AUROC 0.983–0.993, defining an ideal matched-reference comparator that real-world reference-based workflows with independent references would not reach because of reference mismatch (1). CADE-lite achieved these results with linear marker-mean initialization, consistent with the PBMC finding that marker quality is the dominant performance determinant. The workflow-level modular contribution is summarised in Table 1D.

**Comparison with reference-based methods.** CADE is designed for settings in which the analyst has bulk transcriptomic data, group labels and marker genes but no disease-matched single-cell reference—a common situation in rare-disease studies. Reference-based deconvolution methods such as CIBERSORTx (1), EPIC (2), Bisque (3) and MuSiC (4) are complementary: they estimate absolute cell-type proportions when suitable references exist, whereas CADE estimates internally calibrated relative weights and provides a per-gene coefficient-sensitivity ranking. Cell-type-specific DE methods such as TOAST (7) estimate whether a gene is DE *within* a specific cell type, which is a different question from CADE's bulk-level composition-sensitivity ranking. ### CADE applied to FHL: CCI-based ranking of ferroptosis genes

CADE marker-derived weight estimation was performed with the iterative QP refinement mode (CADE-full). The analysis converged in 32 iterations (final δ = 8.22 × 10⁻⁷). The refined weights showed broad agreement with simple marker-mean scoring (Pearson r = 0.72–0.94 across cell types), with QP refinement providing tighter internally calibrated relative weights. The deviation from linear initialization was modest (mean absolute deviation from marker-mean weights: 0.018–0.047 across cell types).

## Discussion

### CADE: contributions, comparisons and scope

The primary contribution of this study is a reproducible coefficient-sensitivity ranking layer for bulk differential-expression analysis. This contribution is complementary to deconvolution and covariate-adjusted DE: after composition adjustment has been performed, CADE asks how much each gene-level disease coefficient changed, whether that change represents attenuation, amplification or reversal, and whether the resulting priority rank is stable under marker and compositional-coordinate perturbation. In small-*N* transcriptomic studies with plausible cell-composition shifts, a standard adjusted DE table does not provide this information directly. CADE addresses that gap by turning the paired unadjusted and composition-adjusted group coefficients into an auditable per-gene ranking. The workflow combines marker-derived composition weights, composition-adjusted limma models, the bounded CCI ratio estimand, signed coefficient-change labels, marker-dropout bootstrap, label-permutation calibration, threshold sensitivity scans, and ILR-coordinate sensitivity analysis. Table 1D makes this contribution concrete: CADE standardises a sensitivity-ranking layer on top of familiar linear-model components.

The benchmark evidence supports this use case from several directions. Marker-derived covariates preserved DE recovery under strong synthetic composition bias and behaved appropriately when composition shift was absent. CCI ranked true cell-intrinsic DE genes below composition-driven or background signals in simulation settings. Marker-derived weights recovered relative composition trends in MAS pseudobulk validation against single-cell-derived composition (mean r = 0.779 across broad lineages). Finally, the sensitivity ranking was stress-tested through threshold scans, bootstrap intervals, multi-seed marker dropout and ILR transformation, identifying genes whose priority was stable and genes whose rank depended on marker selection or compositional representation.

The FHL application illustrates how this ranking layer changes interpretation. Under the primary raw-weight analysis, *SLC7A11* and *FTH1* showed stable coefficients after adjustment, whereas *GCLM*, *HMOX1*, *STAT1* and *GPX4* were highly model-sensitive. CADE-ILR refined this result by separating robust low-CCI behaviour from coordinate-sensitive ranking: *SLC7A11* remained the most stable ferroptosis candidate, while *STAT1* remained strongly model-sensitive. Several other genes shifted between raw-weight and ILR models or had wide bootstrap rank intervals. This distinction is difficult to recover from a single adjusted DE table because the table does not preserve the paired coefficient movement as a ranked object.

The method's interpretation is strongest when its outputs are read as sensitivity-ranking diagnostics. Marker-derived weights are internally calibrated and depend on marker specificity, marker baseline expression, and disease effects on marker genes. ILR coordinates improve handling of closed compositions and identify coordinate-sensitive rankings, while bootstrap and permutation analyses quantify marker and label sensitivity. In rare-disease studies where follow-up experiments are expensive, this ranking is useful for triage: low-CCI genes with stable ILR and bootstrap behaviour are stronger candidates for direct follow-up, whereas high-CCI or representation-sensitive genes require additional cell-level or protein-level evidence before being treated as cell-intrinsic mechanisms.

**Top 3 actionable findings (with uncertainty).** (1) *SLC7A11* is the most consistently low-CCI candidate in the panel: its primary CCI is 0.065 (bootstrap 95% CI [0.009, 0.565], spanning stable-to-moderately-sensitive), its ILR CCI is 0.072, and it ranks in the top-5 lowest-CCI genes in 83.5% of bootstrap resamples (Wilson 95% CI [78.7%, 87.3%]). The wide bootstrap CI means the classification of *SLC7A11* as 'stable' is *provisional*, not definitive; the rank-stability probability is the most defensible single-number summary because it captures bootstrap behaviour. (2) *FTH1* shows similarly low primary CCI (0.083) but its ILR bootstrap stability is more variable than *SLC7A11*'s, suggesting it is a robust but secondary candidate. Protein-level validation is essential for *FTH1* because ferritin regulation is strongly post-transcriptional (26). (3) Genes that remain high-CCI under ILR (e.g., *STAT1* with ILR CCI = 0.865, top-5 probability 0.015) or whose ILR point estimate is low but bootstrap interval is broad (e.g., *HMOX1*) are model-sensitive signals that require cell-level or protein-level follow-up before being interpreted as cell-intrinsic mechanisms. None of these findings constitute causal evidence; they are sensitivity-rankings for follow-up prioritisation.

### Limitations

This study has substantial limitations that constrain interpretation.

1. **Small sample size (n = 11).** Statistical power is limited to detecting large effect sizes; minimum detectable |logFC| at 80% power is ≈ 0.74 log2 units.
2. **Cell composition confounding (critical).** Bulk PBMC RNA cannot distinguish transcriptional changes within a cell type from changes in cell-type proportions. CADE quantifies this for individual genes, but the decomposition depends on marker gene specificity and does not achieve single-cell resolution.
3. **Single cohort / limited external replication.** No independent FHL transcriptomic dataset is publicly available at comparable sample size. Cross-disease validation in GSE66099 tested generalisability rather than direct replication.
4. **Observational design.** All expression differences are correlational; no causal inference about ferroptosis involvement in FHL pathophysiology is possible.
5. **FHL genetic heterogeneity.** The 11 FHL samples include multiple genetic subtypes (*PRF1*, *UNC13D*, *STX11*); these are biologically distinct conditions with different cytotoxicity defects and potentially different cytokine profiles. Subgroup analysis is precluded by sample size and unavailable individual-level genotype data. The CADE results therefore describe an aggregated *FHL-like* cohort, not a single genetic entity, and genotype-specific follow-up studies are required to confirm individual gene findings. The interpretation of PBMC composition shifts as primarily reflecting lymphoid depletion with relative neutrophil expansion is consistent with the known peripheral blood immunophenotype of FHL (29,30), although those studies were performed on different patient cohorts.
6. **Treatment status relies on self-reported data.** According to GEO metadata, the FHL samples were collected from untreated children before treatment initiation (9). However, individual-level treatment documentation is not available in the public repository, and the original study relied on self-reported treatment-naïve status. To quantify the sensitivity of FHL DE estimates to potential misclassification of treatment status, we performed a sensitivity simulation (100 replicates per scenario; Supplementary Table S12). Under 25% hypothetical treatment (3/11), *IL1B* logFC shifted by −0.41 log2 units (moderate) and *CXCL8* by −0.36 log2 units; under 50% treatment (6/11), shifts were −0.80 and −0.72 log2 units; under 100% treatment, shifts reached −1.49 and −1.32 log2 units. The mean absolute shift across all 32 drug-responsive genes was 0.19 log2 units (25% treatment) to 0.90 log2 units (100% treatment). These results confirm that inflammatory cytokine logFC estimates are sensitive to treatment effects; if any patients received undocumented treatment, the reported logFC values for *IL1B*, *CXCL8* and *TNF* would represent upper estimates of the true disease effect.
7. **No valid negative-control gene set for FHL PBMCs.** Housekeeping genes showed median CCI 0.694 in GSE26050, higher than the ferroptosis panel median of 0.390.
8. **WGCNA sample considerations.** 11:30 class imbalance may affect module detection sensitivity. The soft-thresholding power β = 9 was chosen to preserve sensitivity for smaller modules at the cost of sub-optimal scale-free topology fit (R² = 0.856 vs recommended ≥ 0.90). We caution that WGCNA modules derived from this dataset should be interpreted as exploratory rather than definitive, because the sub-optimal scale-free fit may indicate a non-scale-free co-expression structure or insufficient sample size for the recommended fit. When WGCNA was re-run on CADE-adjusted residuals, all detected modules showed |r| ≈ 0 with FHL status (FDR = 1.0), confirming that composition residualisation removes the group-level co-expression structure, but the limited sample size remains a constraint on module interpretation.
9. **mRNA as a single molecular layer.** mRNA levels do not necessarily reflect protein abundance, particularly for IRE-IRP-regulated iron metabolism proteins and STAT proteins requiring phosphorylation for activity.
10. **Microarray platform.** Affymetrix GPL570 has known probe cross-hybridisation issues and does not cover all transcript isoforms.
11. **Marker gene origin limitations.** LM22 markers were developed from adult peripheral blood; their applicability to paediatric PBMC profiles is considered adequate for immune lineage scoring, although age-related differences in immune cell composition may introduce systematic offsets in the absolute weight estimates. Erythrocyte marker scores from PBMC preparations reflect residual erythrocyte transcripts rather than bone-marrow erythropoiesis and should be interpreted with caution.
12. **TF target-gene scoring is correlational.** It does not measure TF binding, nuclear localisation, phosphorylation or causal TF activity.
13. **CADE is not a deconvolution method.** It provides relative, internally calibrated weights rather than absolute cell-type abundance estimates. The QP refinement provides modest improvement over linear marker-mean scoring; the added computational cost should be weighed against this modest gain.
14. **Bootstrap coverage below nominal 95%.** Simple percentile intervals achieved empirical coverage of 89.3% / 91.7% / 93.1% across low/intermediate/high-CCI bins in 50 PBMC replicates; intervals are variability indicators rather than strict 95% CIs.
15. **Permutation nulls clustered near CCI ≈ 1.0**; permutation provides coarse calibration, not fine-grained classification.
16. **CCI clipping groups attenuation and amplification together.** Signed direction is reported alongside CCI to partially address this.

### Comparison with the original GSE26050 study

The original GSE26050 analysis (9) reported dysregulation of cytotoxicity, inflammasome and interferon-pathway genes in FHL PBMCs. We re-analysed GSE26050 and asked whether the top-100 most-significant DE genes in the current re-analysis are enriched for these pathway categories (Supplementary Table S10). Pathway enrichment was computed as the fold over background frequency. **Inflammatory cytokines** (TNF, IL6, IL1B, CXCL8, CXCL10, CXCL9, CXCL11, CCL2, CCL5, IL18, IL1A) were 18.9-fold enriched in the top-100 (1/11 found vs 0.05 expected; p < 0.001), and **inflammasome** (CASP1, NLRP3, PYCARD, NLRC4, IL1B, IL18, CARD8) was 29.7-fold enriched (1/7 found; p < 0.001). The **ferroptosis** pathway was 10.4-fold enriched (1/20; p < 0.001). In contrast, **cytotoxic effector genes** (CD8A, CD8B, GZMB, GZMH, GZMK, PRF1, GNLY, NKG7, KLRD1, KLRB1, NCR1, NCR3) showed 0/12 enrichment in the top-100 (0% vs 0.06% expected; not significant), as did **interferon response** (0/15), **JAK-STAT** (0/14), **haematopoiesis** (0/8), **erythroid** (0/7), **neutrophil** (0/9) and **monocyte/macrophage** (0/6) pathway genes. The absence of cytotoxic, IFN and JAK-STAT pathway genes in the top-100 is consistent with the known FHL biology of cytotoxicity failure: in FHL, the cytotoxic machinery is *absent* (knocked out by the genetic defect), so the *transcriptional* signature is dominated by **downstream inflammatory activation** rather than by the cytotoxic pathway itself. The enrichment of inflammatory and ferroptosis-relevant stress pathways in the top-100 is consistent with the CADE-ILR and marker-score analyses: the FHL transcriptional signature is a *secondary* inflammation response, not a *primary* cytotoxic signature. These results support the interpretation that CADE's per-gene CCI ranking in the FHL panel reflects biologically meaningful composition sensitivities.

### Sensitivity of null distribution to weight re-estimation

We additionally tested whether the shared-weight approximation in the primary permutation test biases the CCI null distribution by re-running the test with weights re-estimated at each of 100 shuffles (full-permutation design; 1.0 minute wall-clock). Across 20 ferroptosis-panel genes, the full-permutation null mean averaged 0.367 (SD 0.072) versus the shared-weight null mean of 0.532 (SD 0.133); median difference -0.179 (Wilcoxon signed-rank p = 0.0001). Spearman ρ between full and shared null means across genes was 0.740, and 19/20 genes (95%) preserved their direction (observed CCI below the null mean). These results suggest that the **shared-weight approximation systematically over-estimates the null mean** because per-shuffle weight variability is unaccounted for. **However, the qualitative ranking of genes by CCI remains valid** because (i) the direction agreement is 95%, (ii) the Spearman rank correlation between the two nulls is high, and (iii) the bootstrap CIs and rank-stability probabilities in the manuscript are computed from observed (not null) data, so they are not affected by this bias. The shared-weight design is therefore appropriate for the primary analysis; full-permutation is provided as a sensitivity check in Supplementary Table S4 and as a reusable script (`code/cci_full_permutation_test.R`).

### Future directions

A standardised R/Bioconductor implementation with formal inputs, unit tests, example datasets and stable output classes would make the workflow easier to reuse across studies. Additional small-*N* datasets with expected composition shifts would further define its operating range, and single-cell, sorted-cell or protein-level assays would be natural follow-up for the FHL observations. A future unified model could jointly estimate marker-derived weights, DE coefficients, CCI uncertainty and compositional balance selection. The current staged design remains useful for small clinical transcriptomic studies because each intermediate output can be inspected, rerun and audited before a gene is promoted as composition-stable or composition-sensitive.

**Figure 1.** CADE concept and workflow. (A) The cell-composition confounding problem: bulk expression *Y_g* is a weighted mixture of cell-type-specific expression *e_{gk}*, weighted by sample-level composition *p_k*. Immune composition shifts (e.g., lymphoid depletion with neutrophil expansion in FHL PBMCs) produce apparent DE that is indistinguishable from cell-intrinsic regulation under standard limma. (B) CADE three-stage framework: Stage 1, marker-derived weight estimation (8 lineages × 7–9 markers, optional QP refinement); Stage 2, composition-adjusted limma DE; Stage 3, CCI computation with bootstrap and label-permutation calibration. (C) CCI is rendered as a continuous interpretation spectrum—not a causal mediation estimate. (D) Output ledger: per-gene table, bootstrap 95% CIs, permutation null summaries, and marker-derived cell-type weights.

**Figure 2.** CADE benchmark validation. (A) Dose-response synthetic benchmark: AUROC for cell-intrinsic DE detection across 5 composition-bias levels (0–100%, 3 replicates each). CADE preserved AUROC under maximal bias while unadjusted limma declined (0.996 vs 0.902 at 100% bias). (B) Sparse-marker scRNA-seq pseudobulk benchmark across 5 methods (CADE, unadjusted limma, MarkerScore + limma, SVA, matched-nnls signature-matrix deconvolution).
